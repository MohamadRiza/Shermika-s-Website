Soaring Through the Glass Ceiling: Accelerating Women in Aviation Leadership

Dr. Shermika Kuweju CMO & Global Tourism Consultant

For decades, the cockpits and executive boardrooms of the global aviation industry have remained predominantly male-dominated spaces. While progress has been made, women climbing the aviation hierarchy continue to encounter unique roadblocks. Overcoming these barriers requires deliberate, systemic changes in how we identify talent and promote leadership.

Having spent over 20 years managing airline sales, expanding cargo channels, and establishing brand positions in competitive markets, I have navigated these challenges firsthand. The aviation sector is an immense engine for global commerce and job creation, but it must offer more equitable spaces for women based strictly on ability and drive.

The "Soaring through the Glass Ceiling" Framework

A recent global study launched by the worldwide aviation industry, titled the "Soaring through the Glass Ceiling" initiative, outlined exactly what holds women back from leadership roles. The study identified five primary inhibitors that corporate boards and airlines must actively address:

  • Lack of upward mobility: Structured limitations in climbing from supervisors to regional executives.
  • Absence of female role models: A scarcity of female executive directors or board members to serve as mentors.
  • Systemic corporate practices: Outdated administrative channels that restrict diverse career paths.
  • Absence of diversity prioritization: Organizations failing to implement robust inclusion metrics.
  • Enduring stereotypes: Persistent biases framing men as naturally "taking charge" and women as "taking care."
"As women, you need to be brave, resilient, and positive. That is how you will climb the ladder of any career path you choose."

Strategies for Systemic Change

To overcome these inhibitors, aviation boards and corporate institutions must establish clear, actionable solutions:

1. Inclusive Talent Pipelines: HR practices should utilize blind-recruitment reviews and balance candidates for managerial posts. Introducing flexible development routes is crucial for balancing domestic life with intense operational timelines.

2. Formal Mentoring Networks: Female leaders must have a seated voice at the table to mentor younger supervisors. In my journey through Thai Airways, Oman Air, and Turkish Airlines, I saw how senior support directly builds the target-oriented, multi-tasking skills required to manage station P&Ls.

3. Recognizing Merit Without Bias: Progressive organizations, like Turkish Airlines, succeed because they reward energetic, dynamic employees based entirely on their yield achievements, commercial sales performance, and motivative contributions, without gender consideration.

Looking Forward

When reflecting on female pioneers, Amelia Earhart remains a core inspiration. Her attitude toward success and failure summarizes the perspective we must hold: "Women must try to do things as men have tried. When they fail, their failure must be but a challenge to others."

By actively dismantling administrative barriers, minimizing biases, and promoting on a platform of pure capability, the global aviation industry can finally clear the skies for the next generation of female leaders to soar.
